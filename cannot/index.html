can’t | cannot

A1, A2, appeal, B2, deductions, guess, MODALITY, NEGATIVE, prediction, reproach

“Can’t” is a contraction of “cannot. Both “cannot” and “can’t” often express the idea of inability, but their formality and context differ. “Cannot” is better suited for formal writing, while “can’t” is commonly used in informal conversations.

The phrase “Yes, you can take a break now.” indicates permission. The speaker is allowing the person to take a break. It’s a straightforward and positive response.

The phrase “I’m sorry, but you can’t use my computer.” indicates a refusal of permission. The speaker is politely declining the request to use their computer. The use of “I’m sorry” adds empathy while still making the refusal clear. It implies that there are specific reasons or rules preventing the person from using the computer.  Similarly, the phrase “People cannot park here.” expresses prohibition. It means that parking your car in the specified location is not allowed.

Let’s explore example sentences using “can’t” and “cannot” to express logical conclusions:

  1. Using “Can’t”:
    • “The suspect can’t be the thief because he has an alibi.”
    • “Given the evidence, it can’t be true that she committed the crime.”
  2. Using “Cannot”:
    • “Based on the data, we cannot conclude that the new drug is effective.”
    • “Considering the circumstances, it cannot be assumed that he acted with malice.”

In both cases, “can’t” and “cannot” are used to logically deduce facts or conclusions. The choice between them depends on formality and personal preference.

Here’s a definition of ‘deduce’ from Google:

arrive at (a fact or a conclusion) by reasoning; draw as a logical conclusion.

‘reproach’

express to (someone) one’s disapproval of or disappointment in their actions.

Here are some negative forms of ‘can’ that you can use to make reproaches or appeals:

    1. “You can’t forget your friends.” (Expressing disapproval or reproach)
    2. “He can’t go on behaving like this.” (Highlighting unacceptable behavior)

‘appeal’

 a serious, urgent, or heartfelt request

“You can’t leave me now. I need you more than ever.”

In this sentence, the speaker is appealing to the other person not to leave because of the urgency and emotional importance of their presence. The use of “can’t” emphasizes the gravity of the situation.

Here’s an example of a guess or deduction that is easy to locate because of ‘I guess‘.  But it is possible that the speaker is expressing disapproval or disappointment too.

I guess you can’t be too picky about who your friends are these days, huh?
