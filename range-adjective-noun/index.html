adjective range in noun phrases

ADJECTIVES, B1, NOUNS, phrases, range

The position of the adjective when placed directly before a noun is referred to being in the attributive position. At lower levels, students already do this so here it becomes important to look at the range of adjectives used to create these noun phrases. One could just leave this to vocabulary if it were not for the fact that the adjective describes the noun and gives that noun a higher level of the language. The problem is, the most frequent adjectives are not the same as the English Vocabulary Profile has listed.

The English Grammar Profile has these noun phrases listed as examples for what it considers to be the range at the B1 level:

main_JJ character_NN1
complicated_JJ situation_NN1
long_JJ dark_JJ hair_NN1
short_JJ fair_JJ curly_JJ hair_NN1
famous_JJ restaurant_NN1
delicious_JJ local_JJ food_NN1
amusing_JJ and_CC entertaining_JJ action_NN1

Yet when we check them at textinspector.com we get A1 adjectives as evidence of B1 level…

main B1 jj character B1 nn1

complicated B1 jj situation B1 nn1

long A1 jj dark A1 jj hair A1 nn1

short A1 jj fair A2 jj curly B1 jj hair A1 nn1

famous A1 jj restaurant A1 nn1

delicious B1 jj local B1 jj food A1 nn1

amusing B1 jj and A1 cc entertaining B1 jj action B1 nn1

In green, the vocabulary is already at B1 level so identifying the phrase for complexity sake has little value. Then there is the parts in yellow which are not straightforward to interpret what they are thinking about at Cambridge.

We can take a guess that they may be using a mathematical equation for the first example: “long A1 jj dark A1 jj hair A1 nn1“. That being able to string together two A1s together doubles their complexity value. As in A1+A1=A2. This is just a guess which we can possibly see some logic behind. However, how can we say that: “famous A1 jj restaurant A1 nn1” could be an example of a greater range of adjectives before a noun?

The only guess is that there must be some kind of cut off even within the A1 proficiency band.

As we investigate, we find that “famous” is listed as the 205th most frequent word tagged *.[JJ] in iWeb corpus, with 914002 instances. Of course, the iWeb is online English and not the whole story on frequency but it does say something about just how rare a word it is, regardless of whether the English Vocabulary Profile has found it to be a word that elementary students use in their output.

Since “range” is quite a vague concept in the EGP it is hard to pinpoint many of the proficiency points of grammar. There is naturally going to seem to be a relationship between the type of vocabulary that can be checked on text inspector at each level to designate the proficiency of writing to some degree, but it also seems there is a more complex unsaid algorithm unrelated to the EVP when it comes vocabulary range within an EGP grammar point. It makes total sense that this is the case too since you can’t designate range just against what other learners can or can’t do. Range in the EGP must be benchmarked against frequency bands of another scale which has not been released to the public.
