ADVERBS, vocabulary

General adverbs are adverbs that can modify a wide range of words or phrases, including verbs, adjectives, other adverbs, and entire clauses. They are often used to indicate manner, degree, frequency, time, place, or purpose.

Here are some examples of the most common general adverbs and how they can be used in sentences:

  • Also: This adverb can be used to add additional information to a sentence. For example, you could say “I went to the store, and I also went to the library.”
  • Just: This adverb can be used to emphasize a point or to indicate that something happened recently. For example, you could say “I just finished reading that book” or “I just got a new job.”
  • How: This adverb is used to ask questions about the manner or degree of something. For example, you could ask “How did you do on the test?” or “How often do you go to the movies?”
  • So: This adverb can be used to indicate the result of something or to emphasize a point. For example, you could say “I studied really hard, so I did well on the test” or “I’m so tired, I could sleep for a week.”
  • Even: This adverb can be used to add emphasis or to indicate that something is surprising. For example, you could say “Even though I was tired, I still went to the party” or “Even the dog was scared of the thunderstorm.”
  • Well: This adverb can be used to indicate the manner in which something is done or to express satisfaction. For example, you could say “I did well on the test” or “The food was well-prepared.”
  • Really: This adverb is used to emphasize a point or to express disbelief. For example, you could say “I really like that movie” or “I really can’t believe that happened.”
  • Still: This adverb can be used to indicate that something is happening despite other circumstances. For example, you could say “I’m still tired, even though I got a good night’s sleep” or “The party is still going on, even though it’s late.”
