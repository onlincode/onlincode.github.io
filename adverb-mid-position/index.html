adverbs in mid position

A2, ADVERBS, auxiliary, emphasis, MODALITY, position

Adverbs in mid position are adverbs that are placed between the subject and the main verb, or after modal verbs, auxiliary verbs, and “be”. This is a common way to place adverbs in English, and it can be used to emphasize the adverb or to add more information about the action.

Here’s an example of an adverb between a modal verb and the main verb + academic collocation:

We would likely address these issues one after the other in a sequential way.

listen to this sentence

Here is an advanced example of a long term intention:

I will always be there for you.

listen

Here are examples of adverbs between the subject and main verb:

I guess you already know that.

listen

The word “already” is used as an adverb to emphasize that something (in this case, knowing something) was completed before now.

I will cover you with love when I next see you.

Lingua Franca

Here’s an example between auxilliary verb ‘HAVE’ and the main verb ‘been’, followed by another existential ‘there’ with modality:

There has never been any distance between us,

 and there never will be.

listen

In the English Grammar Profile, A2 point 30 in the category of ADVERBS:

MID POSITION between the subject and the main verb and after modal verbs, auxiliary verbs and ‘be’.

General points such as these do not specify that there are many other similar points that will either clash at other levels or overlap.  For example:

  • A1 really | always | sometimes
  • A2 adverbs of frequency.
  • A2 adverbs for hedging emphasis
  • B1 ‘can’ + ‘also’, ‘always’, ‘even’, ‘just’, ‘only’, ‘really’, ‘still’

These are just to name a few.  There are more points for passives, modality and perfect verb phrases that all tend to be higher than A2. For this reason, we will not list all the possible forms from expert corpora on this page.
